HW3 - Location in Statistics

Basic definition of Locations in Statistics

In statistics, “location” (or central tendency) refers to a summary value that represents the center or typical value of a dataset. The most common measures of location are the mean, median, and mode, but the concept can be generalized in many ways.

Possibly Infinite Definitions:

By modifying how data is weighted, how outliers are treated, the loss function used, or even the metric space in which data points reside, we can generalize the concept of central tendency in countless ways. These generalizations open up infinite possibilities for defining location or central tendency in both practical and theoretical statistics.

Examples of changes:

Weighted Means with Nonlinear Weights: Assign weights to data points not just based on their frequency or importance, but through arbitrary functions (e.g., exponential, power functions).
Loss Functions for M-estimators: The choice of loss function in M-estimators can be modified infinitely, creating an entire spectrum of robust central tendency measures.
Distance-Based Central Tendencies: In multidimensional data or geometric data, measures of central tendency can be generalized using different distance metrics (e.g., Manhattan, Euclidean, Minkowski).
Information-Theoretic Centrality: The central point of a distribution might be defined in terms of minimizing entropy or maximizing information gain.
Generalized Quantiles: By dividing the distribution in arbitrary ways, any proportion of the dataset can define a form of quantile, extending the concept of “location” beyond the median.
