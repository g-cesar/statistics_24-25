Empirical distribution samples compared to the theoretical distribution

Theoretical distribution values

Mean: \(\mu = \sum_{i=1}^{n} i \cdot p_i = \)0.00
Variance: \(\sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot (i - \mu)^2 = \)0.00

Empirical distributions average

    Samples: 0
    Average of means: \(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bar{x}_i = \) 0.00
    Average of variances: \(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i^2 = \) 0.00

    Disclaimer: statistical calculations are performed on the raw counts rather than normalized values.

    Mean of the Sample Averages

    According to the Law of Large Numbers, as the number of samples increases, the average of the sample means will converge to the mean of the parent distribution. This means that if we take a sufficiently large number of samples, the mean of these sample averages should be very close to the theoretical mean of the parent distribution.

    Variance of the Sample Averages

    According to the Central Limit Theorem, the variance of the sample means is related to the variance of the parent distribution by the formula: \[ \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \] where \( \sigma^2 \) is the variance of the parent distribution and \( n \) is the sample size. This implies that the variance of the sample means decreases as the sample size increases. However, when considering the average of the sample variances, we expect it to be close to the theoretical variance of the parent distribution, especially as the number of samples increases.

    Practical Implications

    In practice, when we calculate the average of the sample means and the average of the sample variances, we should observe the following:

    Convergence of Means: The average of the sample means should be very close to the theoretical mean of the parent distribution, validating the Law of Large Numbers.

    Consistency of Variances: The average of the sample variances should be close to the theoretical variance of the parent distribution, confirming that our samples are representative of the parent distribution.

    This relationship is crucial in statistical analysis as it helps in understanding the behavior of sample statistics in relation to the population parameters.
