HW4 - Independence in Statistics

Basic definition of Independence concept in Statistics

Statistical independence is a fundamental concept in probability theory, representing the idea that the occurrence of one event does not affect the occurrence of another. In simpler terms, two events are considered independent if knowing the outcome of one does not provide any information about the other. This concept extends to various contexts in probability and statistics and is essential in areas such as statistical inference, decision theory, and stochastic processes.

Formal Definition of Independence:

Let A and B be two events in a probability space. The events A and B are said to be independent if: \[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]

Analogies in Probability Theory:

Coin Flips: Consider two independent coin flips. Let A be the event that the first flip results in heads, and B the event that the second flip results in heads. Since the result of the first flip does not influence the second, we have: \[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25 \] This demonstrates independence because each flip is unaffected by the other.

Dice Rolls: If we roll two fair dice, the outcome of one die (say, getting a 3) is independent of the outcome of the other die (say, getting a 5). This is because the probability of any specific outcome on each die is the same, regardless of the outcome on the other.
